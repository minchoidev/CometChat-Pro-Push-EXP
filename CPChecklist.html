Home Safety, Falls Prevention and Patient Assessment

When conducting Ad Hoc or Circle of Care Home visits, it is important to not only assess the patient, but also to assess for illness and injury prevention. There are many factors that have an effect on a person's health and safety such as physical ability, vision, environment and medications. The following checklists are assessment tools you can use to assist you in determining safety, physical ability and a patient's risk of falling with the goal to assist the patient develop personal safety and falls prevention strategies.

Points to consider when providing assessments and utilizing these checklists
  • Every year, one in three Canadians 65 years of age and over experience a fall
  • The majority of falls resulting in death for elders happen inside the home (%60)
  • Injuries caused by falls are the #1 reason for injury hospitalizations for persons 65 years of age and older
  • More serious injuries increase with age--adults 85 or older who fall are 4 to 5 times more likely to be injured than people age 65-74
  • People who are admitted to a hospital because of a fall tend to stay in hospital for a very long time, an average of 33 days
  • Falling IS NOT a normal part of aging
  • Risk of falling increases after an elder is discharged home form the hospital due to muscle weakness, pain, new medications and/or new assistive devices
  • Pets (dogs, cats etc) increase an elders chance of falling
  • Even when injuries are minor, experiencing a fall can have a significant negative impact on an elder's sense of well-being, safety, and independence
  • Being afraid of falling causes many elders to limit their activities and contributes to more falls
  • Most falls can be prevented
  • Walking/simple exercise can assist in falls prevention
Patient Mobility Assessment



Timed Up and Go Test

Simple test used to identify persons that are at risk of falling due to balance or gait problems. This test should only be conducted for patients who can walk unassisted with or without assistive devices. Note: the assessment is not a race and if you believe the assessment cannot be conducted safely, do not implement the test.

Instructions to Administer Test

  1. Measure a 3m (10') distance from a chair and mark the point. Show this point to the individual before beginning the test.
  2. If the individual wears eyeglasses or uses an assistive device such as a cane, walker etc. they should do so while performing the test.
  3. Instruct the individual to rise from a chair that has a straight back.
  4. Ask the individual to rise from the chair by stating 'ready, set, go' and begin timing.
  5. Observe the patient as he/she walks to the mark, turns and walks back to the chair.
  6. Stop timing when the patient sits back down in the chair.
  7. Record the time it took the patient to complete this exercise. This is the patient's score.
